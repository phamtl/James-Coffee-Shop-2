Every beans has its story...

Have you ever wonder how much skill it takes to create a happy little cup of coffee in your hand right now? No matter what your answer is, we will still show you the skillful process from the farm to your cup, let's get ready!

1. Planting Seeds

A coffee bean is actually a seed. When dried, roasted and ground, it’s used to brew coffee.

2. Harvesting The Cherries

There is typically one major harvest a year. The cherries may be picked either by hand or by machine. There are two types of harvesting:

3. Processing The Cherries

The beans are passed through several processing machines, then rinsed by going through additional water channels, and are ready for drying.

4. Drying The Beans

These beans can be sun-dried by spreading them on drying tables or floors, or they can be machine-dried in large tumblers. The dried beans are warehoused in jute or sisal bags until they are ready for export.

5. Milling The Beans

Before being exported, parchment coffee is processed in the following manner: Hulling, Polishing and Grading.

6. Exporting The Beans

The milled beans are loaded onto ships in either jute or sisal bags loaded in shipping containers, or bulk-shipped inside plastic-lined containers.

7. Coffee Tasting

Coffee is repeatedly tested for quality and taste. This process is referred to as cupping and usually takes place in a room specifically designed to facilitate the process.

8. Roasting The Coffee

Roasting transforms green coffee into the aromatic brown beans. The beans are kept moving throughout the entire process to keep them from burning. After roasting, the beans are immediately cooled either by air or water.

9. Grinding The Coffee

The objective of a proper grind is to get the most flavor in a cup of coffee. How coarse or fine the coffee is ground depends on the brewing method. For example, Espresso machines use 132 pounds per square inch of pressure to extract coffee.

10. Brewing The Coffee

There are a lot of ways to brew coffee; however, we are going to introduce a quite unpopular method to the world, the Vietnamese way of brewing coffee.

Traditionally, Vietnamese coffee is brewed inside a phin filter. They are simple ceramic or stainless steel coffee filters with holes punched out of the bottom. It’s worth a notice that any drip coffee filter will work for brewing a Vietnamese coffee.
